From the edge of the Arctic
Story by Nicole Casal Moore. Multimedia by Marcin Szczepanski
Sep. 21, 2016
A Michigan engineer is tracking the planet’s vital signs from the epicenter of climate change. |Long read

Brie Van Dam lifts the sled rope and clicks its harness around her waist. The sled is loaded with the tools she’ll need to gather a day’s worth of data about the Arctic snowpack. Poles in hand, she muscles off from Toolik Field Station — a world-renowned climate change research outpost. Her purple shadow leads the solitary operation into the white expanse.

Skis squeak against the powder for a mile and a half to a plot where wooden stakes in the tundra mark where she’ll take measurements.

Snow is vital to the Arctic, and the planet as a whole. In this ecosystem, it’s an insulator and humidifier of the ground all winter, keeping plants and animals alive, even if dormant. It’s a store of fresh water that replenishes springtime soils. And on a global scale, it’s a heat shield, reflecting some of the sun’s energy back to space and helping to regulate Earth’s temperature.

“Van Dam has been connected to the snow one way or another for much of her life — from building igloos with her brother as a child to writing a dissertation on how sunlight reacts with pollutants in the spaces between fallen snowflakes. Now, as manager of Toolik Field Station’s Environmental Data Center, her wintertime duties include monthly checks of the camp snow, including its depth, density and liquid water content. Regular measurements are essential to understand how a system is changing. Researchers from all over the world with projects at TFS can use the data Van Dam gathers to put their own findings in context.

When Van Dam and her sled arrive at the first sampling site, she throws a down jacket on top of her other layers. She won’t be as active here as she was on her commute, and temperatures are in the teens. That’s cold by most standards, but not here. It’s been warm at TFS this year.

Measuring snow depth is straightforward. She dips a scientific ruler into the snowpack at 50 different points and records the millimeter markings in a journal. She moves on to density measurements, unpacking a hollow metal tube with a t-shaped grip called a “Federal corer.” She drives it in, twists it, and lifts out a snow plug. She pours the contents into a plastic bag.

Then she does it again. And again. Twenty cores came out at this “dry heath” tundra location.

“One of the things I really love about snow is it tells you stories. You can sort of read it,” she had said earlier in the day, as she called us over to a trail of bird footprints. Snow patterns can tell her which way the wind is blowing, for example, and based on that, she knows what grows underneath it. It also holds clues about TFS wildlife.

“If you look right here,” Van Dam pointed out, “the ptarmigan was walking walking walking, and you can actually see the wing prints from when it took off.

“Isn’t that beautiful?”
